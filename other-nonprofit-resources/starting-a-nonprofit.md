# Starting a Nonprofit (501(c)(3))

Forming a nonprofit is one of the most meaningful steps you can take to protect and grow your mission. This guide walks you through the full process in the correct order, along with what you need to keep your organization in good standing for the long haul.

> **Quick note:** This guide is meant to orient you, not replace legal advice. For anything complex, see [Nonprofit Legal Resources](nonprofit-legal-resources.md) for guidance on finding affordable legal help.

---

## Step 1: State Incorporation

You must incorporate at the **state level first**, before applying to the IRS. Incorporation creates your organization as a legal entity.

### What You Need

* **Articles of Incorporation:** The founding document you file with your state. It must include your organization's name, purpose, and a dissolution clause (what happens to assets if you close). The IRS requires specific language for 501(c)(3) eligibility.
* **Registered Agent:** A person or service with a physical address in your state who can receive official legal and government mail on your organization's behalf.
* **Filing Fee:** Varies by state, typically $25 to $100. Many states offer reduced fees for nonprofits.

**Where to file:** Your state's Secretary of State office. Search "[your state] Secretary of State nonprofit incorporation" to find the right form.

### After Incorporating

Once your articles are approved, you receive a certificate of incorporation. Keep this document safe; you will need it for the IRS application and bank accounts.

---

## Step 2: Draft Your Bylaws

Bylaws are your organization's internal rulebook. You need them before you can complete the IRS application. They should cover:

* Purpose of the organization
* Board structure (number of directors, terms, voting rules)
* Officer roles (Executive Director, President, Secretary, Treasurer, etc.)
* How meetings are called and conducted
* Conflict of interest policy
* Amendment process

---

## Step 3: Form Your Board of Directors

Your board provides governance and legal oversight. The IRS and most states have requirements:

* **Minimum board size:** Most states require at least 3 directors.
* **Independence rule:** Per IRS guidance, **no more than 49% of your board members can be related to each other** by family (spouse, sibling, parent, child, etc.) or close business relationships. This ensures your board can make independent decisions free of conflicts of interest. See [IRS Publication 557](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p557) and the IRS Form 1023 instructions for details.
* Directors should represent your community and mission, not just the founding team.

---

## Step 4: Get an Employer Identification Number (EIN)

An EIN (also called a Federal Tax ID) is required before you apply to the IRS for tax-exempt status. You need it to open a bank account and hire staff, too.

**Apply free at:** [irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)

The process takes about 15 minutes online and your EIN is issued immediately.

---

## Step 5: Apply for Federal 501(c)(3) Status

With your EIN, articles of incorporation, and bylaws in hand, you can apply to the IRS for tax-exempt status.

### Choose the Right Form

| Form | Who It's For |
|---|---|
| **Form 1023** | Most organizations. Required if you expect gross receipts over $50,000/year in any of the first 3 years. |
| **Form 1023-EZ** | Small organizations expecting under $50,000/year. Shorter and cheaper to file. |

**File online at:** [pay.gov](https://www.pay.gov) (search "Form 1023")

**Filing fees:** $275 for Form 1023-EZ; $600 for Form 1023 (as of 2024; verify current fees at [irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/application-for-recognition-of-exemption)).

### What to Expect

Processing times vary. Form 1023-EZ approvals often take a few weeks. Full Form 1023 applications can take several months. The IRS may send follow-up questions; respond promptly to avoid delays.

Once approved, you receive a **determination letter** confirming your 501(c)(3) status. This is one of your most important documents. Store it permanently.

### Public Benefit Purpose

Your application must clearly describe a public benefit purpose. 501(c)(3) organizations must operate for one of these exempt purposes: charitable, religious, educational, scientific, literary, testing for public safety, fostering national or international amateur sports competition, or preventing cruelty to children or animals.

---

## Step 6: Apply for State Tax Exemption

After you receive your IRS determination letter, apply for state income and sales tax exemption in your state. Each state has its own process:

* **State income tax exemption:** Usually handled through your state's Department of Revenue or Department of Taxation.
* **Sales tax exemption:** Often a separate application. Allows you to make purchases without paying sales tax on qualifying items.

Search "[your state] nonprofit tax exemption" or "[your state] 501(c)(3) state exemption" for the correct forms.

## Ongoing Compliance Requirements

Forming your nonprofit is the beginning, not the finish line. Here is what you need to do every year to keep your status in good standing.

### Annual IRS Filing (Form 990)

All 501(c)(3) organizations must file with the IRS each year. Which form you file depends on your revenue:

| Annual Gross Receipts | Form Required |
|---|---|
| Under $50,000 | **Form 990-N** (e-Postcard, filed online, free) |
| $50,000 to $200,000 | **Form 990-EZ** |
| Over $200,000, or total assets over $500,000 | **Form 990** (full form) |

**Filing deadline:** The 15th day of the 5th month after your fiscal year ends (May 15 for a December 31 fiscal year end). Extensions are available.

**Critical:** If you miss three consecutive years of 990 filings, the IRS automatically revokes your tax-exempt status. Reinstatement is possible but costly and time-consuming.

### State Annual Reports and Registration Renewals

Most states require nonprofits to file an annual report with the Secretary of State to keep the organization in good standing. Fees and deadlines vary by state. Missing filings can result in administrative dissolution.

Check your state's Secretary of State website each year.

### Board Meeting Minutes

Boards are required to hold regular meetings and keep written minutes on file. Minutes should document:

* Date, location, and attendees
* Decisions and votes taken
* Any conflicts of interest disclosed
* Actions assigned

Minutes do not need to be exhaustive, but they must exist. Store them permanently; they may be requested by auditors, lenders, or state regulators.

### Financial Records Retention

Keep the following records for the periods noted:

* **Permanent:** Articles of incorporation, bylaws, IRS determination letter, board meeting minutes, tax returns (990s)
* **7 years:** General financial records, bank statements, grant agreements, contracts
* **3 years:** Non-permanent HR records

The IRS can audit up to 3 years back in most cases, and up to 6 years if it suspects substantial underreporting.

### State Charitable Solicitation Registration

If your organization solicits donations (in person, by mail, online, or through third parties), most states require you to register as a charitable solicitor. This is separate from your tax exemption and your annual report.

* Requirements vary widely: some states exempt small organizations, some require registration before your first ask.
* If you fundraise online, you may need to register in multiple states.
* [Charitable Allies](https://charitableallies.org/) and the [National Council of Nonprofits](https://www.councilofnonprofits.org/) both have state-by-state registration guidance.
